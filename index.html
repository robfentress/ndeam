  5. Section 504 prohibits programs and activities receiving Federal financial assistance from discriminating against individuals with disabilities.   6. If people are interested, you can read directly the relevant parts of the law:

  7. Reveal next fragment
  8. Read: Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act defines requirements for information technology accessibility (including, but not limited to, web accessibility) for Federal agencies and those receiving federal funds
    1. Section 508 lists specific requirements in terms of accessibility for information technology.
    2. Not limited to the web
    3. Has been harmonized with W3C standard for accessibility, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines
  9. Reveal next fragment
  10. Read: The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) seeks to ensure equal access to programs, services, and activities for people with disabilities.
    1. Title II of the ADA generally requires that state and local governments provide qualified individuals with disabilities equal access to their programs, services, or activities unless doing so would fundamentally alter the nature of their programs, services, or activities or would impose an undue burden. And in case you were wondering, that burden refers to the organization as a whole, in our case, Virginia Tech, rather than your individual department.
      1. Title II covers state and local while Section 504 of Rehab Act only applies if an entity receives federal assistance
    2. Title III on the other hand applies to private entities and requires that places of public accommodation be accessible. This is an evolving area of the law, but, in at least some cases, legal settlements suggest this applies to web sites.
  11. Reveal next fragment
  12. Read: Depending on your country, state, or locality, there may be additional legal requirements. For instance, in Virginia, the Information Technology Access Act, among other things, requires that state agencies ensure that any information technology they use be accessible to the blind and visually impaired.
  13. If people are interested, you can read directly the relevant parts of the law:
    1. § 2.2-3502. Assurance of nonvisual access.: In general, the head of each covered entity shall ensure that information technology equipment and software used by blind or visually impaired employees, program participants, or members of the general public (i) provide access (including interactive use of the equipment and services) that is equivalent to that provided to individuals who are not blind or visually impaired; (ii) are designed to present information (including prompts used for interactive communications) in formats adaptable to both visual and nonvisual use; and (iii) have been purchased under a contract that includes the technology access clause required pursuant to § 2.2-3503.
    2. § 2.2-3503. Procurement requirements.: B. At a minimum, the nonvisual access standards shall include the following: (i) the effective, interactive control and use of the technology (including the operating system), applications programs, and format of the data presented, shall be readily achievable by nonvisual means; (ii) the technology equipped for nonvisual access shall be compatible with information technology used by other individuals with whom the blind or visually impaired individual interacts; (iii) nonvisual access technology shall be integrated into networks used to share communications among employees, program participants, and the public; and (iv) the technology for nonvisual access shall have the capability of providing equivalent access by nonvisual means to telecommunications or other interconnected network services used by persons who are not blind or visually impaired. A covered entity may stipulate additional specifications in any procurement.

  • Accessible Rich Internet Applications 1.1 (WAI-ARIA)

  1. Read: In addition to laws governing accessibility, there are defined standards for what it means to be "accessible".
  2. Reveal next fragment, WCAG.
  3. Read: The first of these is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines or WCAG.
  4. WCAG is the internationally-recognized standard that explains how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.
  5. It was developed by the Web Accessibility Initiative of the World Wide Web Consortium, which is the same overarching organization responsible for HTML and other web standards.
  6. Reveal next fragment, WCAG 2.1.
  7. Read: On June 5, 2018 WCAG 2.1 came out, which is a significant update to the standard, expanding provisions for mobile, low vision, and cognitive and learning disabilities.
  8. Reveal next fragment, WAI-ARIA.
  9. Web pages have become more complex over time, such that some of them function essentially like desktop applications. The Web Accessibility Initiation-Accessible Rich Internet Application standard, sometimes referred to as WAI-ARIA, or Way-ARIA, or just plain ARIA.
  10. Accessibility of web content requires semantic information about widgets, structures, and behaviors, in order to allow assistive technologies to convey appropriate information to persons with disabilities. This specification provides an ontology of roles, states, and properties that define accessible user interface elements and can be used to improve the accessibility and interoperability of web content and applications. These semantics are designed to allow an author to properly convey user interface behaviors and structural information to assistive technologies in document-level markup.
  11. Reveal next fragment, ATAG.
  12. Read:Beyond ensuring that web content is accessible, it is important that the tools used to create that content are, themselves, accessible, which is what the Accessible Tool Authoring Guidelines, abbreviated as ATAG, are designed to address.
  13. ATAG provides guidelines for designing web content authoring tools that are both more accessible to authors with disabilities (Part A) and designed to enable, support, and promote the production of more accessible web content by all authors (Part B).

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

  • 4 Design Principles:
    Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust
    • Contain 12 Guidelines
      • Testable success criteria
        • Each criteria at level A, AA, AAA
  1. Read: The most important standrd of the ones I listed on the previous slides is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, so let's spend a little time digging into what that
  3. Perceivable: Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.
  4. Operable: User interface components and navigation must be operable.
  5. Understandable: Information and the operation of user interface must be understandable.
  6. Robust: Content must be robust enough that it can be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

WCAG Example

  • Principle 1 – Perceivable: Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

  • Guideline 1.3 – Adaptable: Create content that can be presented in different ways (for example simpler layout) without losing information or structure.

  • Success Criterion 1.3.1 – Info and Relationships (Level A): Information, structure, and relationships conveyed through presentation can be programmatically determined or are available in text.

Here is an example of the hierarchy of Principles, Guidelines, and Success Criteria. The Principle is that users must be able to perceive things. A Guideline within that principle is that the user should be able adapt the content so it can be viewed in different ways And a specific Success Criterion is that the content be structured using appropriate semantic elements so that the information about and relationships between elements on the page don't depend on specific sensory and physical abilities in order to perceive.

Link text

Link text should describe purpose.

  • Avoid "click here" or "read more".
    • "Read more about x" is better.
  • Image links describe purpose too
<a href="arbor-day.html">
  <img scr="tree.jpg" alt="Arbor Day Sale">
</a>
<a href="arbor-day.html">
  <img scr="tree.jpg" alt="">
  <span class="sr-only">Arbor Day Sale</span>
</a>
  1. Read: Because screen reader users can navigate by jumping from link to link using a key command, it is important that the text of any links describes their purpose. For instance, where the link takes the user or the name of the document that would be downloaded. This saves these users time by letting them quickly navigate to where they need to go.
  2. Reveal the first fragment
  3. Read: However, if the links contain more generic text, such as "Read more", especially if there are multiple "Read more" links, this affordance is less useful.
  4. Reveal the next fragment
  5. Read: Rather than saying "read more" or "click here", it is better for the link text to say "read more about x"
  6. Reveal the next fragment
  7. Read: This same principle is at play when an image is the only content of a link. Usually, the text alternative for an image should be a description of the image. However, if you did that in this case, the text that would be spoken would be the image's alt text, which often will not describe the link's purpose.
  8. Reveal the next fragment
  9. Therefore, in those instance, you can change the alt text to describe the purpose of the link.
  10. Reveal the next fragment
  11. Alternately, you could set the image's alt attribute to the empty string, which causes it to be ignored by screen readers, and then provide additional text that is visually hidden but spoken by the screen reader, which descripbes the link's purpose.

Reference

Data tables are used to organize data with a logical relationship in grids. Accessible tables need HTML markup that indicates header cells and data cells and defines their relationship. Assistive technologies use this information to provide context to users. Header cells must be marked up with TH, and data cells with TD to make tables accessible. For more complex tables, explicit associations may be needed using scope, id, and headers attributes.

  8. Read:
    • One other way to magnify things that doesn't require separate software is to use your browsers native zooming functionality.
    • For instance in Safari I can magnify the page by pressing Command + + or choosing Zoom In from the View menu.
    • This will make the text bigger, but can cause problems with horizontal scrolling unless you are using responsive design techniques that transform the layout as the viewport size decreases, which is essentially what is happening when you use Browser Zoom.
  9. Read:
    • Some people may not want to zoom the entire page, and may prefer to just make the text on the page bigger. For instance, you can set your preferred text size in Chrome's preferences window.
    • This should not cause content to reflow, as with browser zoom, and, therefore, this may be a preferable strategy for some users.
    • However, unlike browser zoom, this technique only works if the page has been coded to use relative units for font size. These relative units include em, rem, %, and keywords like large.
    • However, if you use pixels, points, or inches as your units when setting font size, because these are absolute measures, they will not change when the user sets their preferred text size in their browser's preferences.
    • Another thing to be careful of it that your page design will accommodate having the user set their text to be up to 200% of its normal size, without any of the text becoming truncated or overlapping other context and thus becoming unreadable.
